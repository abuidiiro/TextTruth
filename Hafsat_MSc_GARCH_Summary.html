Modelling and Forecasting Nigeria Exchange Rate Volatility Using GARCH Models

MSc Thesis Summary

Abstract: This study investigates exchange rate volatility in Nigeria using GARCH and EGARCH models across multiple currencies. Empirical findings reveal persistent volatility, strong ARCH effects, and superior performance of EGARCH models. Contrary to conventional financial theory, a positive leverage effect is observed.

1. Introduction

Following the 2023 exchange rate unification policy, Nigeria transitioned to a managed float regime. This resulted in heightened exchange rate volatility, with significant depreciation of the Naira and rising inflationary pressures.

3. Methodology

3.1 Return Series

\[ r_t = \ln\left(\frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}\right) \tag{1} \]

4. GARCH(1,1) Model

\[ \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2 \tag{2} \]

Volatility persistence is given by \( \alpha + \beta \).

5. EGARCH Model

\[ \ln(\sigma_t^2) = \omega + \beta \ln(\sigma_{t-1}^2) + \alpha \left(\frac{|\varepsilon_{t-1}|}{\sigma_{t-1}} - \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\right) + \gamma \frac{\varepsilon_{t-1}}{\sigma_{t-1}} \tag{3} \]

The parameter \( \gamma \) captures asymmetry (leverage effects).

6. Empirical Results

  • High persistence (\( \alpha + \beta \approx 1 \))
  • Significant ARCH effects across currencies
  • EGARCH provides superior fit
  • Evidence of positive leverage effect

7. Conclusion

The study confirms that GARCH-family models effectively capture exchange rate volatility in Nigeria. However, the presence of a positive leverage effect suggests deviations from traditional financial market behavior, warranting localized modelling approaches.
